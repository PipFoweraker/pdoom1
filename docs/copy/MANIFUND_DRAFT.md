## 1. What is p(Doom)1?

p(Doom)1 is a nightmarishly hard, weird side project where players attempt to
battle an inexorable rise in, you guessed it, p(Doom). I heard someone say "write
what you know" and took it literally.

[glue] At its core, the game is about the challenge facing real AI safety
researchers, grantmakers and philanthropists, wrapped in a game to communicate the
challenge while mitigating the actual despair. You can't win -- you can only buy
time. There's no victory condition; your score is how long you hold P(Doom) back
before a run ends.

I started designing it out of a confluence of my interests (game design), gaming
background (Magic: the Gathering, PoE, Tarkov, Civ, X:COM, sim games), professional
background (project management specialising in governance), and career (operations
work in Global Catastrophic Risks).

## 2. What are this project's goals? How will you achieve them?

- Get the game in front of more eyeballs by improving quality of UX, UI, and
  infrastructure.
- Turn that into community engagement at a higher level of background technical
  understanding -- people learning a bit more about AI safety as a side effect of
  an engagingly hard game, the same way young me learned a bit about history by
  playing Civilization.
- [glue] Underneath it is a real theory of change: people voluntarily adopting
  good actions generates the space for better actions to come about -- it's how
  small and middle-power players affect things, through norm- and market-setting.
- Secret goal: watch people argue about how p(Doom) is calculated.

## 3. How will this funding be used?

- Fund development so I can continue to justify spending some of my time on the
  game.
- Pay a developer to take over leading some elements of gameplay.
- Pay human artists to uplift and steer the artistic direction.
- Pay for assets and hosting.
- Support any community that emerges out of people enjoying the game.
- Maintain and update the open historical AI-safety dataset the game runs on. It
  lives in a standalone public repo, is broadly useful to researchers, journalists
  and developers, and is designed to be available for other people's games and
  tools to draw from -- with maintenance and update schedules built into the dev
  cycle.

## 4. Who is on your team? What's your track record?

Me, presently. I designed a board game about Rugrats trivia when I was 8 -- my
brother and I enjoyed playing it, though my parents sucked at answering the tricky
questions. I played, then judged, then did coverage for Magic: the Gathering, and
during that time interviewed hundreds of players, many pro-level, who went on to
lead and design games themselves -- talking a lot about game design and balance,
and getting a good understanding of what makes depth and complexity work across
rulesets. [glue] Professionally I do operations work in Global Catastrophic Risks
with a governance-focused project-management background, so the subject matter
isn't a costume.

## 5. What are the most likely causes and outcomes if this project fails?

- People might take AI safety less seriously due to this game being low-status or
  failing in reputationally damaging ways.
- People might see this and be discouraged from making a better game.
- The game might contribute to ideas that are dangerous or discouraging of good
  actions -- like giving a false impression that some capabilities are maintainable.
- [glue] I've designed against that last one deliberately: the game is explicit
  that we're not saying or implying labs really do the truly weird stuff that takes
  place later in play, and the underlying dataset stays strictly factual (what
  happened, with sources), never predictive.
